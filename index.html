The real mandate

Be senior enough to advise. Practical enough to execute. Disciplined enough to scale across clients.

Bravo positions itself as an embedded fractional operations team that owns mission-critical work, combines hands-on execution with senior guidance, and uses playbooks developed across 225+ brands. That makes the Director role a three-sided job: client operator, engagement owner, and multiplier of junior capacity.

Bespoke client reality vs. reusable Bravo playbooks

Pattern recognition is valuable only when it accelerates diagnosis without forcing the wrong answer onto a different stage, channel mix, or partner network.

Strategic leadership vs. tactical credibility

The Director must discuss working capital and roadmaps with founders, then still be willing to audit an invoice, review inventory, or unblock freight when the situation demands it.

Founder trust vs. operational accountability

An embedded operator has to be empathetic to the founder's pressure while making missed commitments, decision delays, and weak ownership visible enough to change.

Multi-client breadth vs. consequential depth

Managing 2-4 brands means senior attention must be allocated deliberately around consequence, reversibility, SOW commitments, and team leverage - not whoever pings loudest.

Candidate thesis
“The goal is not to become indispensable to the client. It is to help build an operating system the client can trust.”

That changes how I would approach a Bravo engagement: enter with tactical empathy, diagnose before prescribing, stabilize the consequences first, codify only what should repeat, and measure the handoff as seriously as the intervention.

First 90 days

Earn trust. Reduce surprise. Build only what the client can sustain.

Bravo advertises senior operating support designed to move the needle in 90 days. My entry plan is built around visible learning, consequence-first stabilization, and client-owned mechanisms.

0-30

Read & earn trust

  • Shadow current cadences and tactical work.
  • Map demand, supply, inventory, cash, partners, data, and decision rights.
  • Confirm SOW outcomes and hours model.
  • Surface the top service, cash, margin, and partner risks.
  • Create a shared learning agenda with the client and Bravo team.
31-60

Stabilize & instrument

  • Install short exception and escalation cadences.
  • Clarify ownership for constraints and partner recovery plans.
  • Repair the few data definitions that block useful decisions.
  • Build the initial client scorecard and S&OP decision calendar.
  • Delegate repeatable execution while coaching junior team members.
61-90

Build & transfer

  • Codify stage-appropriate mechanisms and standard work.
  • Document decision rights, escalation thresholds, and partner SLAs.
  • Train the client and Bravo operators who own daily execution.
  • Review outcome evidence against the SOW and budget.
  • Agree the next-state roadmap: maintain, deepen, or hand off.
The hard objection

“You do not come from a traditional CPG career.”

True. I would not ask Bravo to ignore that gap, and I would not manufacture brand experience I do not have.

My case is transferability under consequence: current growth-stage operations leadership across production, purchasing, quality, systems, and customer-facing work; high-velocity Amazon operations; long-cycle manufacturing and quality leadership; distributed logistics and fulfillment; and a record of making complex work visible enough to improve.

The ramp I would owe Bravo is specific: CPG retailer mechanics, category-specific forecasting patterns, co-man/co-packer economics, chargeback and routing-guide details, shelf-life/MOQ dynamics, and Bravo's own playbooks. My operating advantage is that I know how to learn a system without disrespecting the people already carrying it.

Executive questions

Questions I would bring to Anders and the leadership team
